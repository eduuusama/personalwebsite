No One Creates Alone: The Power of Place

Understanding Innovation Through Historical Examples

February 21, 2024

Let's start by defining what innovation is: any act, idea, or product that changes an existing domain, or that transforms existing domain into a new one. It is important to remember, however, that a domain cannot be changed without the explicit or implicit consent of a field responsible for it.

If innovation is more than personal insights and is cocreated by domains, fields, and persons, then creativity can be constructed, deconstructed, and reconstructed several times over the course of history.

The most important implication of a system model is that the level of innovation in a given place and at a given time does not depend only on the amount of the individual. We are used to thinking that innovation begins and ends with a person, it is easy to miss the fact that the greatest spur to it may come from changes outside the individual.

Innovation in Collaboration - Florence 1400s

A good example is the sudden spurt in artistic creativity and innovation that took place in Florence between 1400. These were the golden years of the renaissance, and it is generally agreed that some of the most influential works of art in Europe were created during that quarter century.

If innovation is something entirely within a person, we would have to argue that for some reason an unusually large number of creative artists were born in Florence in the last decades of the fourteenth century. Perhaps some freak genetic mutation occurred, or a drastic change in the education of Florentine children suddenly caused them to become more creative. But an explanation involving the domain and the field is much more sensible.

No other place matched Florence in the intensity and depth of artistic achievement. Why is that so?

The explanation is that the field of art became particularly favorable to the creation of new works at just about the same time as the rediscovery of the ancient domains of art. Florence had become one of the richest cities in Europe first through trading, then through the manufacture of wool and other textiles, and finally through the financial expertise of its rich merchants.

It was an atmosphere of wealth and uncertainty that the urban leaders decided to invest in making Florence the most beautiful city, by commissioning great frescoes and majestic statues, they must have felt that they were weaving a protective spell around their homes and businesses.

The important thing to realize is that when the Florentine bankers, churchmen, and heads of great guilds decided to take their city intimidatingly beautiful, they did not just throw money at artists and wait to see what happened. They became intensely involved, in the process encouraging, evaluating, and selecting the works they wanted to see completed. It was because leading citizens, as well as the common people, were so seriously concerned with the outcome of heir work that the artists were pushed to perform beyond the previous limits.

Innovation in Isolation - US 1940s

In 1941, John Mauchly made a pivotal four-day visit to John Vincent Atanasoff's laboratory in Iowa State College. Atanasoff had been working in relative isolation on an electric computing machine since 1937. During these four days, Mauchly examined Atanasoff partially completed computer, the ABC (Atanasoff-Berry Computer), discussed technical details, and reviewed Atanasoff extensive documentation. The visit would later become the center of a historic patent dispute, as elements of Atanasoff design appeared in ENIAC, the computer Mauchly claimed his ENIAC design was independently conceived, his exposure to Atanasoff ideas during those four days in Iowa proved crucial to the development of electronic computing.

Atanasoff's story demonstrates how isolation can limit innovation. While he conceived an electronic computer in Iowa, his physical and intellectual isolation severely constrained his ability to fully develop the idea. In contrast, when Mauchly visited Atanasoff in 1941, he was already part of a vibrant scientific community at the University of Pennsylvania. After seeing Atanasoff's work, Mauchly could build upon those ideas within a rich ecosystem of engineers, technicians, and mathematicians - particularly collaborating with Presper Eckert. Had Atanasoff been working within such a community rather than alone in Iowa, he may have overcome the technical challenges that left his computer only partially complete. His story reinforces that groundbreaking innovation rarely emerges from solitary genius, but rather from the collision and refinement of ideas within supportive networks.

The AI Revolution - San Francisco Now

Currently something special is happening in San Francisco. I believe building a company in the office 5+ days / week in SF gives you the highest probability of building a generational tech company. The talent density is second to none and it's where your first customers most likely be.

Innovation is usually a group effort, involving collaboration between visionaries and engineers, and that creativity comes from drawing from many sources. Only in storybooks do inventions come like a thunderbolt, or a lightbulb popping out of the head of a lone individual in a basement or garage.

These historical examples and my current experience in San Francisco reassure me that it's the best place for me, the company, the team, and innovation appetite. While individual brilliance matters, it's the ecosystem — the combination of skilled people, supportive institutions, and cultural dynamics — that transforms ideas into revolutionary changes.
