Embrace The Problem, Not The Solution

A Guide to Problem-First Innovation

March 2024

When starting a new company, as innovators we look for something new and exciting. This can be inspired by other new technologies, discoveries, markets or trends, and could potentially fall into the trap of having a solution and later looking for a problem to solve with it. Here are some ways to prevent this:

Describe the problem in 5 words or less. The fewer buzz words (which are usually just trendy) the better. Another great way of defining the problem is by filling in this phrase: We solve A for B by doing C. This has to be clear to all parties involved, founders, team, investors and clients. By this time you should have enough understanding of the space, the target audience and the market.

Start with the smallest version possible. Really distill into what is the MVP and core function of your solution. To the bare bones. What about your product will be undeniably unique and will be the main cause for retention for customers. Hit the most important problem first and hard.

Validate hypothesis. If the solution you came up with is actually solving an existing problem, there will be interest right away. This does not have to be a full software development, a landing page that receives interest could be enough. This can also save you time and effort before building a full MVP.

Remain open to change. One of your greatest assets at this time of your journey is the adaptability and readiness to change course. Any action that limits this agility can swiftly undermine your chances of success. With a perfectionist mindset, you try to create elegant automated systems that operate in harmony, but this is not a worthy goal at this time. Focus on launching a viable product by any means necessary and getting it to your customers. The time for refining will come later, with a larger team and more resources.

It's understandable for technologists and innovators to be enthusiastic about developing novel, cutting-edge solutions. However, it's vital to anchor these innovations in genuine, practical and real-world problems. Many fall in love with the solution and not the problem. While it's natural for engineers and innovators to be excited about building new, innovative products, it's crucial to ensure that these creations are grounded in real-world problems. And although it's really easy in theory, it's fucking hard. But hopefully by keeping these principles as a guiding pole, I can avoid falling into investing a bunch of time into something that is pretty cool but ultimately useless for people.
